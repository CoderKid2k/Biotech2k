Rules to Stay Disciplined

When it comes to staying disciplined to my strategy, I love to make up rules. I love to give them catch names that makes them easy to remember.

1/Don't Put All Your Eggs in One Basket

This is probably a rule everyone has heard about in investing. It is the rule about being diversified and not putting all my money into one company or one investment. This rule is about managing my risk in case one company becomes the next Theranos. I know some poeple will put more money in companies they have the highest conviction in and less money in those companies they find more risky. I tend to do things different. I like to risk a little to make a lot. I don't have to swing for the fences with every pitch. I tend to have at least 10 companies and like to have about 20 so I am not putting more that 5% in a single company.

2/Gambling is for Vegas

One of the biggest risks I see in the market is the Get Rich Quick mentality. I have never seen anyone get rich quick, but I have seen a lot of people get wiped out in a flash. When it comes to investing, I do not gamble. I do not go all in on one highly speculative company no matter how much I believe in it. I don't play binary events like clincal readouts. I don't use margin and I don't play options. I tend to use a very simple strategy of value investing. If I want to gamble, I will go to Vegas where its is far more fun and entertaining. I know a lot of people think investing is gambling, but its not. You have information and data in investing that allows you to make educted decisions about companies. Not getting swept up in the Fear of Missing out or euphoria of the market is a discipline of its own.

3/Never Risk more than You can Afford to Lose

I mentioned this before in the right sizing of investment positions. I can't tell you how many people I come across that are all in on 1 company or 1 investment. It blows my mind that they are so confident to lose all their money if one event happens that disrupts that investment. Diversification goes hand in hand with Position Sizing. People who are retired should not be 80% in speculative biotech or some new fad crypto coin. I see way too many investor who focus only on the reward and completely dismiss the risk. I go by the idea of manage the risk and the rewards will take care of themselves. I never risk more than 5% of my own money on any single company.

4/The Starship Trooper Rule

This is one of my favorite rules. It comes from the movie where the capatian says, "The job is yours until you die or I find better". This rule is important to remember when investing. Its about keeping good companies for as long as they remain good companies. I see too many investors turn an investment into a trade. They buy a company and it pops 50%. They sell it all. Then they miss out on the next 300% it goes up because they had the investment right, but they sold it for a trade. I never sell out of a company unless if fails or I find better. This rule keeps me in the winners for the long term.

5/The 3 Stooges Rule

This rule is about the value of management. Watching some companies management is like watching an episode of the 3 Stooges. It would actually be funny if it were not losing investors money. I will never own a company were I have any doubts about the management. I will often sell a company should the management change. That depends on my position and my belief in the previous management.

6/I Would Rather Pay up for Success than be Overweight Failure

This is my key rule for biotech investing. Many investors will expect everything to go right for their highly speculative company. They will buy a big position into data expecting a good read out. Any failure costs them a ton of money. I tend to reward success. I like to keep my positions small and buy more of companies that have good data while avoiding the ones that have bad data. That develops my portoflio naturally to be weighted to the winners. These companies have a long road to commercial success. Waiting for the data might miss a quick pop, but it will be far from over.

7/The Kenny Roger Rule

You got to know when to hold them and know when to fold them. This rules is about being truthful with myself and admitting I got a company wrong. This rule allows me to get rid of the bad companies before they turn little losses into big losses. Sometimes, getting out of a company isn't about them going bad. Sometimes the company has just fully matured. This is when the company's future does not seem like it could drive a much higher valuation. I can think of a few companies that reached a point where I could not see a future that was brighter than their past. They were fully played out.

8/When in Doubt, Sell it Out

This is an important rule for me. Sometimes, I become unsure of a company. Maybe its the management or maybe its an event like data. Something just happens that makes me question the company. My rule is to sell out of the company and wait 30 days. The 30 days is the wash sale rule in case I am taking a loss on that company. I can at least lock in that loss for taxes. After 30 days, I can assess again and buy them back if I find it wasn't an issue. I find a lot of investors feel like they are not investors if they walk away from a company for while. There is no merit badge for loyaly in investing.

9/Never Buy all At Once

This rule reminds me I don't have a cyrstal ball. Most of investing is ruled by emotions which make us do the opposite of what we should be doing. We get excited by the rallies and want to load up the truck. We get scared into the sell offs and don't ever want to buy. I set a rule to buy a little each time the stock pulls back a good 10% to 20% from my last purchase. In this age of investing, I can tade with no fees at all. There is no reason for me to buy a huge position all at once.

10/Playing With the Houses Money

This rule is about remembering to take profits so that I can pay off my investment with the profits. Nothing is better in investing than owning something for free. The goal, for me, is to take profits into the bull markets so I can pay off my core position. Then I can buy back more shares into the next bear market.

11/The 10th Man Rule

This is a newer rule for me. I used it for many years, but I did not ever consider it a rule. The 10th man is a concept I took from World War Z. I know it was an official rule before then, but that is when I adopted it as an official rule. This states that if you have 10 people and they all agree on something, the job of the 10th man is to disagree with the other 9 and try to prove them wrong. I find this to be the most powerful rule. This is about remaining objective and not getting caught up in the emotions or excitement. I use social media every day. When I make a statement that is bullish or bearish about the biotech sector or a biotech company, I can get immediate feeback. When everyone agrees with me, I have to assume I am wrong. When no one agrees with me, I have to assume I might be right.

* I am not a Financial Advisor. This is just My Strategy. Please refer to your financail advisor before making any investing decisions.
